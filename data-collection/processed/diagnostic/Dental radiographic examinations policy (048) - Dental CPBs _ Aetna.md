# Dental radiographic examinations policy (048)

**Number:** 048
Revised

**Subject:** Dental radiographic examinations

**Reviewed:** March 13, 2026

## Policy

Necessity and appropriateness of dental radiographic images will be determined through review of submitted services and their alignment with the guidelines outlined in the American Dental Association/Food &amp; Drug Administration report, "Dental Radiographic Examinations: Recommendations for Patient Selection and Limiting Radiation Exposure." An additional position and policy with clinical recommendations was released in January 2026. "American Dental Association and American Academy of Oral and Maxillofacial Radiology patient selection for dental radiography and cone-beam computed tomography." Consistency between the 2012 and 2026 publications is the conclusion that the need for radiographic images will be assessed by weighing the potential diagnostic yield against the risk of radiation exposure as related to clinical conditions.

Background

Radiographic images can help the dental practitioner evaluate and definitively diagnose many oral diseases and conditions. However, the dentist must weigh the benefits of taking dental radiographs against the risk of exposing a patient to x-rays, or ionizing radiation, the effects of which can accumulate from multiple sources over time and the lifetime of the patient.

Dentists should only order radiographic images when they expect that the additional diagnostic information obtained will affect patient care. This approach will optimize patient care, minimize radiation exposure and responsibly allocate all health care resources.

Radiographic screening for the purpose of detecting disease before clinical examination or evaluation should not be performed. A thorough clinical evaluation, consideration of the patient medical and dental history, **review of any prior dental** radiographic images, caries and/or periodontal risk assessment and consideration of both the dental and the general health needs of the patient should **precede all** radiographic imaging. Furthermore, radiographic images **should not be taken at regular intervals** (e.g. every recall appointment) without a diagnostic rationale based on the aforementioned assessment. The number of r **adiographs** or radiographic images exposed **MUST** be based on the results of examination, and not based on standing orders (inappropriate to require full mouth images for all new patients, or recall images for every recall). The routine exposure of cone beam CT scans for every patient as part of the intake examination process, is not appropriate unless there is a documented pathologic condition or concern that was discovered as part of the initial clinical examination or evaluation. CBCT or CT Scans are not indicated for caries detection or screening. The suspected pathologic condition or anatomic concern should be documented in the clinical record as justification for exposing the patient to the CBCT or CT Scan, prior to such exposure.

Once a decision to obtain images or radiographs is made, by the dentist it is the dentist's responsibility to follow the ALARA Principle (As Low as Reasonably Achievable) to minimize the patient's exposure to radiation. Examples of good radiologic practice include:

- proper film exposure and processing techniques
- use of protective aprons and thyroid collars, when required by law
- limiting the number of images obtained to the **minimum necessary** to obtain **essential diagnostic information**

In dental plans that cover dental radiographic images, the available benefit **may be limited** as to the number and/or frequency of images, in keeping with ADA/FDA guidelines. Accordingly, the benefit for other intraoral or extraoral dental images may be limited as well. Image capture only codes are not benefitted to the same practitioner associated with the interpretation of those images (example, CDT code D0391).

Practices should not have standing orders for exposing images for patients (new, existing or recall). Selection of the type of image and how many images should be based on the Dentist's recommendation obtained after the examination or evaluation is performed and documented in the clinical record. A number of States have laws that require documentation of the examination or evaluation in the clinical record prior to exposure of the patient to ionizing radiation.
